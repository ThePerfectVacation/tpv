Eiffel Tower History: The Beginning

The genesis of the Eiffel Tower lies in the preparations for the 1889 World's Fair (Exposition Universelle) held in Paris to commemorate the centennial of the French Revolution. The competition for the fair's centerpiece was fierce, but Gustave Eiffel's bold and innovative design, initially met with skepticism, ultimately won. His vision was not just for a tall structure, but for a symbol of modern engineering and French ingenuity that would draw the world's attention to Paris.

This ambitious project involved hundreds of skilled workers meticulously assembling over 18,000 prefabricated wrought iron components. These pieces were held together by an astounding 2.5 million rivets, a process that required specialized expertise and coordination. The innovative use of hydraulic elevators, designed by Otis, was also groundbreaking, allowing visitors to ascend to unprecedented heights.

Facing Skepticism: Early Reactions to the Eiffel Tower

Interestingly, the Eiffel Tower was not universally loved upon its completion. Many prominent artists, writers, and intellectuals of the time vehemently opposed its construction, viewing it as an unsightly industrial intrusion that would forever mar the classic beauty of Paris. They penned passionate letters and signed petitions against what they derisively called a "gigantic black smokestack" and a "half-built factory" dominating the city skyline. More Than a Monument: How the Eiffel Tower Became Permanent

Initially intended to be a temporary structure, slated for demolition after just 20 years following the 1889 World's Fair, the Eiffel Tower's unexpected value for scientific research proved to be its saving grace. Its impressive height made it ideal for early experiments in wireless telegraphy (radio transmission) by pioneers like EugÃ¨ne Ducretet. The tower's strategic importance for communication became undeniable, ensuring its permanent place in the Parisian landscape and transforming its perception from a temporary exhibition piece to an indispensable part of the city's infrastructure and identity.

Eiffel Tower Essentials: Key Facts and Figures

  • Height: Approximately 330 meters (1,083 feet) with antennas, and around 300 meters (984 feet) without. Its height has varied over the years with the addition of different antennas.
  • Weight: Over 10,000 tons (approximately 10,100 metric tons).
  • Levels: Three main levels accessible to the public, each offering a distinct experience and perspective.
  • Construction Material: Primarily wrought iron, chosen for its strength-to-weight ratio and flexibility against wind.
  • Number of Visitors: Millions annually, consistently ranking it among the most visited paid monuments in the world, a testament to its enduring global appeal.

Navigating the Levels: What to See on Each Floor

Each of the three levels of the Eiffel Tower offers a unique perspective and a distinct set of experiences, making each ascent worthwhile.

First Floor: Experiencing the Void

The first floor, at 57 meters (187 feet), features a relatively new and thrilling attraction: a transparent floor section that provides a vertigo-inducing view directly down to the ground below. This level also houses interactive exhibits detailing the tower's history and construction, a souvenir shop for mementos, and the contemporary Madame Brasserie restaurant, offering a relaxed dining experience with impressive views.

Second Floor: Breathtaking Views of Paris

The second floor, situated at 115 meters (377 feet), is arguably the most popular level for its truly breathtaking panoramic views of the entire city of Paris. From this vantage point, you can easily spot and identify other famous landmarks such as Notre Dame Cathedral, the Louvre Museum, the SacrÃ©-CÅ“ur Basilica, and the Arc de Triomphe. This level also features observation telescopes for closer looks and the renowned Michelin-starred Le Jules Verne restaurant, offering an exquisite dining experience high above the city.

Third Floor (Summit): Reaching the Top

The third floor, or summit, soaring at 276 meters (906 feet), provides the most spectacular and expansive views of Paris and its surrounding areas, stretching for dozens of kilometers on a clear day. This level houses Gustave Eiffel's original office, meticulously preserved with wax figures depicting him and his daughter, offering a glimpse into the tower's past. For a truly memorable experience, you can also enjoy a glass of champagne at the dedicated champagne bar while taking in the unparalleled vistas. Access to the summit is via a separate elevator from the second floor.
